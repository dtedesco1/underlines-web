# State Dept. Freezes New Student Visa Interviews for Social Media Vetting Roll-out

A 27 May 2025 cable from Secretary of State Marco Rubio ordered every U.S. embassy to stop adding new interview slots for student and exchange visas while universal social-media screening rules are finalized.

#### Focusing Facts

- Directive dated 27 May 2025 instructs posts to remove all unclaimed F, M and J visa appointments and await further guidance before scheduling new ones.

- Rubio told senators the State Department has already revoked “probably in the thousands” of student visas, up from about 300 reported in March 2025.

- NAFSA estimates international students contributed $43.8 billion to the U.S. economy and supported 378,000 jobs in the 2023-24 academic year.

#### Context

Washington has periodically tried to police the politics of would-be visitors—from the 1918 Alien Act used during the first Red Scare, to the McCarran-Walter Act of 1952 that barred suspected communists, to the 2017 ‘Muslim-ban’ era social-media questionnaires.  The new freeze echoes those episodes: ideological scrutiny is again being justified as national-security vetting, this time targeting pro-Palestinian speech.  Long-term, the order sits at the intersection of two structural forces: the digitization of identity (governments mining online footprints) and the century-old U.S. strategy of attracting global talent to sustain scientific and economic primacy.  Restricting that pipeline risks accelerating a trend already visible since the Trump-era visa slowdowns and Covid-19—students and research funding drift toward Canada, Europe and Australia.  If repeated or made permanent, today’s pause could be remembered like the 1924 Immigration Act: a short-sighted gating of human capital that reshaped global power dynamics for decades; if reversed quickly, it may register as another brief swing in America’s recurring pendulum between openness and nativist anxiety.

#### Narrow Perspectives

- **International wire services & public broadcasters (Reuters, BBC, POLITICO)**: They present the halt as an administrative pause so the State Department can widen social-media security vetting of all foreign student visa applicants in response to antisemitism and terrorism concerns. By relying heavily on State Department cables and officials’ quotes, their reporting largely adopts the administration’s security framing and gives limited space to civil-liberties criticism or university anxieties. Focusing on free-speech and financial harms, these outlets foreground critics and may underplay the administration’s stated security rationale, reflecting their generally adversarial stance toward Trump policies.
