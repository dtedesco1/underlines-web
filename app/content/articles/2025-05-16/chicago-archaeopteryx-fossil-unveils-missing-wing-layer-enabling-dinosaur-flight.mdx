# Chicago Archaeopteryx Fossil Unveils Missing Wing Layer Enabling Dinosaur Flight

A newly prepared Field Museum specimen shows, for the first time, long tertial feathers on both upper arms of Archaeopteryx, closing the wing-body gap and confirming the 150-million-year-old dinosaur could generate lift, albeit chicken-style.

#### Focusing Facts

- The specimen, acquired in 2022 and CT-scanned/UV-mapped for over 1,300 hours, is the first complete Archaeopteryx where tertial feathers are preserved and documented.

- The pigeon-sized fossil retains 24 tail vertebrae and nearly intact 3-D bones; its data set is the first full Archaeopteryx CT scan released openly to researchers.

- Soft-tissue traces show mobile first and third fingers plus ground-adapted toe pads, indicating terrestrial habits with short powered flights.

#### Context

In 1861 the flattened Bavarian Archaeopteryx specimen became the Victorian era’s ‘smoking gun’ for Darwin’s just-published theory, much as Raymond Dart’s 1924 Taung skull later anchored human evolution.  Today’s revelation echoes that moment: advanced imaging resurrects a fossil long trapped in private hands, reminding us that technology and access—not new digs alone—drive paradigm shifts.  The find fits a century-long pattern: incremental discoveries keep pushing powered flight earlier and show it likely evolved multiple times in dinosaurs, challenging the linear ‘march to birds’ narrative.  On a 100-year horizon, digitized open scans may matter more than the feathers themselves, democratizing paleontology the way the 1953 DNA model democratized genetics; the Chicago Archaeopteryx could thus mark a pivot from fossil scarcity to data abundance, altering how we test deep-time evolutionary questions far into the 22nd century.
