# Witkoff-Putin Kremlin Meeting Two Days Before Trump’s Aug 8 Ukraine Ceasefire Ultimatum

On 6 Aug 2025, U.S. envoy Steve Witkoff flew to Moscow and held direct talks with Vladimir Putin, a last-minute attempt to secure a Ukraine ceasefire before President Trump’s 8 Aug deadline for sweeping secondary sanctions on Russian energy trade.

#### Focusing Facts

- Witkoff landed at Vnukovo-2 at 07:00 local time on 6 Aug 2025 and was escorted by Russian Direct Investment Fund chief Kirill Dmitriev to a Kremlin meeting with Putin later that morning.

- Trump’s ultimatum threatens tariffs of up to 100 % on countries purchasing Russian oil—starting with India—if fighting is not halted by 23:59 ET on 8 Aug 2025.

- Russia captured roughly 886 sq mi of additional Ukrainian territory from Dec 2024 to Jun 2025, reinforcing Putin’s belief he can withstand new pressure, according to the Institute for the Study of War.

#### Context

Flash ultimatums delivered by envoys have a mixed record: think Neville Chamberlain’s 1938 mission to Munich that bought months, or Henry Kissinger’s 1972 shuttle to Hanoi that failed to coerce North Vietnam despite massive U.S. bombing.  Trump’s sanctions-first brinkmanship reflects a longer trend since 2014 of Washington substituting financial leverage for direct force, while Russia, buffered by wartime autarky and BRICS energy outlets, wagers that economic pain is tolerable so long as battlefield gains continue.  Whether this hurried Kremlin meeting produces more than an “air truce,” it crystallises a structural shift: global energy flows are hardening into rival blocs, and U.S. ability to cow third-party buyers is increasingly contested.  On a century scale the episode may be less about one ceasefire than about how durable post-Cold War American economic primacy remains when confronted by a sanctions-hardened, resource-rich adversary and an ambivalent Global South.
