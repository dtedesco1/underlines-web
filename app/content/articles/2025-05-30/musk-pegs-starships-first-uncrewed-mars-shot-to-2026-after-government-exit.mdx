# Musk Pegs Starship’s First Uncrewed Mars Shot to 2026 After Government Exit

On 29 May 2025, Elon Musk publicly reset SpaceX’s schedule, saying Starship will attempt an uncrewed Mars transfer during the late-2026 launch window, two days after the vehicle’s ninth test flight failed and a day after he quit the Trump administration.

#### Focusing Facts

- Musk slated the Mars launch for the “end of 2026,” the next Earth-Mars opposition that allows a 7–9-month transit.

- He gave the plan only a “50-50” likelihood and said a miss would postpone the attempt to 2028.

- Starship Flight 9 disintegrated 30 minutes after liftoff on 27 May 2025, cancelling a planned livestream update.

#### Context

Industrialists setting audacious space deadlines is hardly new: in 1961 John F. Kennedy pledged a moon landing by 1970, and Wernher von Braun’s 1969 Integrated Program Plan sketched human Mars flights by 1986—deadlines that slipped or died when budgets, politics, and engineering realities collided. Musk’s 2026 date echoes that tradition, but it also rides two deeper 21st-century currents: the shift from state-led to billionaire-financed exploration and the growing reliance on rapid, data-rich prototype failure loops borrowed from software culture. His simultaneous resignation from a short-lived government-efficiency post underscores how private actors increasingly bypass slow public institutions. Whether Starship reaches Mars next year or in 2035 may not matter on a century scale; what could matter is the precedent of a privately controlled, reusable heavy-lift system capable of leaving Earth orbit. If successful, it might open cislunar and planetary space the way clipper ships opened global trade in the 19th century; if it stalls, it will join the long list of unrealized Mars timetables, reminding future planners that visions outpace propulsion chemistry and orbital mechanics.
