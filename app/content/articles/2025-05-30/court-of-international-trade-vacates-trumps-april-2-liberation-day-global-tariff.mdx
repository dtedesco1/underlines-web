# Court of International Trade Vacates Trump's April 2 “Liberation Day” Global Tariffs

On 28 May 2025 a three-judge panel of the U.S. Court of International Trade unanimously struck down and permanently enjoined President Trump’s sweeping April tariffs on nearly all imports, ruling he exceeded the authority granted by the 1977 IEEPA.

#### Focusing Facts

- Ruling orders the April 2 tariff proclamations—10 % on all imports and higher rates on 180+ countries—“vacated and their operation permanently enjoined.”

- Judges held that only Congress may levy duties and that IEEPA’s emergency powers do not delegate “unbounded” tariff authority to the president.

- Lead lawsuit was filed by the Liberty Justice Center for five small importers; at least six other suits and 12 states have similar challenges pending.

#### Context

Executive tariff overreach has triggered judicial push-back before—Franklin Roosevelt’s attempted rubber-stamping of New Deal price controls met the Schechter Poultry rebuke in 1935, and Richard Nixon’s 10 % import surcharge of 1971 collapsed once the emergency rationale faded.  The 2025 ruling fits a century-long oscillation between congressional and presidential control of trade: post-1945 liberalization via GATT/WTO ceded ground after 2016 to unilateral duties justified as security measures.  By re-asserting the non-delegation principle, the trade court signals that even in today’s populist, protectionist moment, the constitutional guardrails around taxing power still bite.  Whether Congress seizes this opening to rewrite tariff statutes or the executive finds new work-arounds will shape the next hundred years of global commerce and U.S. separation-of-powers doctrine—echoing how the 1930 Smoot-Hawley Act and its backlash rewired trade policy for decades.
