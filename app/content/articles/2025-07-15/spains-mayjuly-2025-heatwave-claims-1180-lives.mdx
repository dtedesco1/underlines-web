# Spain’s May–July 2025 Heatwave Claims 1,180 Lives

Spain’s Environment Ministry says 1,180 people died from heat between 16 May and 13 July 2025—up from 70 in the same 2024 window—as the country issued 76 red-alert days for temperatures above 40 °C.

#### Focusing Facts

- 1,180 heat-attributed deaths registered nationally from 16 May–13 July 2025, ministry citing Carlos III Health Institute.

- 76 red heat alerts were issued in the period, compared with zero during the same span in 2024.

- Four traditionally cool northern regions—Galicia, La Rioja, Asturias, Cantabria—reported the highest mortality spikes.

#### Context

Europe has seen lethal hot summers before: the 2003 heatwave killed an estimated 70,000 people and the 2022 season another 61,000, but those events struck mainly central and southern France and Italy; Spain’s 2025 surge north of the Cantabrian Mountains echoes the 1911 UK heatwave that shocked a normally temperate zone. The episode highlights two structural curves: (1) a steadily warming Iberian Peninsula—mean summer temperatures have risen ~1.7 °C since 1961—and (2) an ageing Spanish demographic (20 % over-65) whose physiology and fixed-income housing make adaptation harder. Methodological caveats remain—Spain’s “MoMo” excess-mortality model infers rather than certifies heat deaths—yet the 17-fold year-on-year jump still signals growing exposure outpacing public-health defences. On a 100-year horizon, such events test the resilience of Mediterranean agriculture, energy grids, and pension-heavy healthcare systems; if century-old records continue to fall, Spain’s north could resemble today’s Andalucía, forcing economic and migratory realignments across Europe.
