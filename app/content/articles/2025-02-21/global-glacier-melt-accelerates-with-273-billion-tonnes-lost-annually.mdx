# Global Glacier Melt Accelerates with 273 Billion Tonnes Lost Annually

Between 2000 and 2023, an extensive study combining satellite and field measurements confirms that glaciers worldwide are melting faster, losing an average of 273 billion tonnes of ice each year and accelerating 36% in the recent decade.

#### Focusing Facts

- Globally, glaciers have lost about 6,542 billion tonnes of ice from 2000 to 2023, contributing 18 mm to sea-level rise.

- Regional declines are stark, with European Alps glaciers shrinking up to 39-40% and New Zealand glaciers shrinking by 29% over the period.

- Ice loss rates increased from 231 billion tonnes/year (2000-2011) to 314 billion tonnes/year (2012-2023).

#### Context

This comprehensive assessment, published in Nature and coordinated by the World Glacier Monitoring Service, integrates diverse observational techniques — from ground measurements and optical to radar and gravimetric satellite data — reminiscent of mid-20th century breakthroughs in climate monitoring but now revealing an unprecedented acceleration in melt. Historically, glacier studies provided early warnings of anthropogenic climate impact; however, these new figures underscore a systemic shift in the cryosphere's response to rising greenhouse gas emissions. Such accelerated ice loss not only threatens regional freshwater supplies and increases coastal flood risks but also foreshadows long-term global shifts in sea-level trends and climate dynamics over the next century. The scale and speed of these changes, when compared to previous eras, demand immediate and coordinated policy responses to mitigate further irreversible damage.
