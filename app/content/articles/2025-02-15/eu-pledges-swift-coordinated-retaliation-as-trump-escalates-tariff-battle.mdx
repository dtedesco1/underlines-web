# EU Pledges Swift, Coordinated Retaliation as Trump Escalates Tariff Battle

In February 2025, following President Trump's announcement of new reciprocal tariffs, the EU Commission vowed a firm and immediate countermeasure to protect its trade interests.

#### Focusing Facts

- US steel and aluminum tariffs of 25% are set to take effect on 12 March 2025.

- Trump’s reciprocal tariff policy, based on country-by-country assessments, could roll out additional measures as early as 2 April 2025.

- Diverging views within the EU: while France pushes for a hawkish, rapid response, Germany, Italy, and Hungary favor a more measured, negotiation-focused approach.

#### Context

This episode echoes the tariff tussles of 2018 when Trump’s earlier tariffs on EU steel and aluminum provoked retaliations that temporarily subsided under the Biden administration. Like the economic disputes ignited by the Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act of 1930—which contributed to deepening global economic disarray—this renewed conflict highlights the enduring struggle between protectionist impulses and the principles of open global trade. Over a period of 100 years, such disputes have repeatedly tested the resilience of international trade systems. Although this clash might seem like another instance of political brinkmanship, its long-term significance depends on whether it catalyzes structural changes in global trade norms or settles into the recurring cycle of retaliatory measures.

#### Narrow Perspectives

- **EU Official and Pro-Retaliation Voices**: They condemn Trump’s reciprocal tariffs as unjustified challenges to free and fair trade and insist on an immediate, firm counteraction to defend a rules-based global trading system. In emphasizing a swift and forceful response, they may overstate threats to EU economic stability to strengthen internal resolve and international negotiating leverage. - **EU Internal Debates on Retaliation Strategy**: This camp recognizes the need for a response but displays internal divisions, with some members advocating for a rapid, decisive action while others prefer a more measured, negotiative approach to avoid escalation. Their nuanced stance may reflect national economic and political interests, leading to inconsistent messaging that could undermine a unified EU response.
