# Q1 2025 US GDP Falls 0.3% After Tariff-Driven Import Surge

Advance BEA data show the economy flipped from 2.4% growth to a –0.3% annualized decline in Q1 2025 as firms rushed imports before Trump’s April 2 tariff hike, marking the first negative print since early 2022.

#### Focusing Facts

- Goods imports soared 41.3% annualized, slicing roughly 5.0 percentage points off headline GDP.

- Core PCE inflation quickened to 3.5% in Q1 from 2.6% in Q4 2024.

- Trump’s April 2 order raised the effective U.S. tariff rate to its highest level since 1930.

#### Context

Sudden output dips tied to tariff front-running echo the 1930 Smoot-Hawley rollout, when importers accelerated purchases in late 1929 before duties took effect—only to see GDP slide 8.5% in 1930.  A century later, the episode highlights two durable forces: (1) the fragility of statist trade swings in a supply-chained world, and (2) the statistical quirks of GDP accounting that can misread inventory-laden import spikes as domestic weakness.  Since the 1990s, U.S. growth has been increasingly synced to cross-border value chains; each lurch toward protectionism (Nixon’s 10% import surcharge in 1971, Trump’s 2018 steel duties) has produced short-term distortions, but this is the first time such a shock coincides with high public debt, aging demographics, and post-pandemic inflation.  On a 100-year horizon, today’s –0.3% may look trivial, yet it could mark an inflection where the U.S. tacitly trades a century of liberal-trade leadership for episodic autarky, testing whether modern service-heavy economies can afford to weaponize tariffs without eroding the very demand they seek to protect.
