# Google Cloud IAM Glitch Cascades Into Global Multi-Service Outage (12 Jun 2025)

A permissions failure inside Google Cloud’s Identity & Access Management layer about 2 p.m. ET on 12 June 2025 briefly crippled Google Workspace and scores of dependent sites, causing the largest cross-platform outage since 2021 before partial recovery two hours later.

#### Focusing Facts

- Downdetector logged 13,000+ Google Cloud incident reports at 11:30 a.m. PT, while Spotify alone saw 40,000 user outage submissions.

- Google’s status page time-stamped the root incident at 10:51 a.m. PT and said every GCP region except us-central1 was affected by 13 separate product failures.

- By 4:07 p.m. ET Google reported full recovery for all services except Meet, though Cloudflare and AWS were still posting intermittent errors.

#### Context

Concentrated cloud control has failed before—AWS’s 14-hour US-East-1 collapse on 22 April 2011 and Facebook’s 6-hour BGP misroute on 4 Oct 2021 both halted swathes of the internet—but today’s IAM malfunction cut across three major providers simultaneously, exposing new inter-provider dependency loops. The episode underscores a decade-long drift toward hyperscale monoculture: in 2010 no single vendor hosted more than 5 % of web traffic; by 2025 Google, AWS and Cloudflare together steward roughly 90 %. Each episode of downtime therefore behaves less like an isolated IT hiccup and more like a systemic utility failure—akin to the 1965 Northeast blackout that forced regulators to rethink grid redundancy. On a 100-year horizon, the tension between efficiency through centralization and resilience through decentralization will shape whether the internet evolves into a brittle “super-grid” or a mesh of interchangeable micro-clouds; June 2025 will be cited as another warning shot that redundancy must keep pace with scale.
