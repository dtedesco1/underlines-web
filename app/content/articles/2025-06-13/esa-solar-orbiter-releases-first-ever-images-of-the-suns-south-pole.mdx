# ESA Solar Orbiter Releases First-Ever Images of the Sun’s South Pole

On 11 June 2025 ESA unveiled photographs shot in mid-March from 17° below the ecliptic, giving humanity its first direct view of the Sun’s south pole and its chaotic magnetic field.

#### Focusing Facts

- Images were captured 16–17 Mar 2025 when Solar Orbiter was ~25 million miles (40 million km) from the Sun at 17° south heliographic latitude.

- Magnetograms revealed intermixed north- and south-polarity patches at the pole, evidence of an imminent field reversal during the current solar-maximum phase.

- The probe’s orbit will tilt to 24° in Dec 2026 and 33° in Jun 2029, setting up progressively higher-latitude observations.

#### Context

Solar Orbiter’s snapshots echo NASA/ESA’s Ulysses (1994–2008), which first flew over the poles but, lacking a camera, left them literally unseen.  The new imagery caps decades of engineering aimed at escaping the energy-efficient ecliptic plane—an ambition reminiscent of Voyager 1’s 1977 gravity-assist slingshots that opened the outer solar system.  Strategically, it advances a long trend toward three-dimensional heliophysics: accurate space-weather prediction for power grids, satellites, and crewed exploration, a need far more acute than during the 1859 Carrington super-storm when telegraph wires were the only vulnerable tech.  A century from now, such polar monitoring could underpin routine solar forecasting—or serve as a historical waypoint showing how early 21st-century scientists first stitched a complete magnetic portrait of our star.
