# Argentina Locks in $20B IMF Bailout to Spur Market Reforms

Argentina secured a staff-level agreement with the IMF for a $20 billion Extended Fund Facility, marking a pivotal step in President Milei's overhaul of currency controls and fiscal policies amid mounting social dissent.

#### Focusing Facts

- The IMF deal includes an initial disbursement of about $12 billion upfront over a 48‐month program.

- The package comes as Argentina abandons strict currency controls, shifting to a flexible trading band of 1,000–1,400 pesos per dollar.

- Argentina, having undergone 23 IMF programs and owing over $40 billion, continues a long history of external bailouts.

#### Context

This agreement is reminiscent of earlier neoliberal reforms such as those of the 1990s, when Argentina and other Latin American nations adopted market-friendly policies to counter chronic deficits and instability. Unlike the 2001 crisis triggered by unsustainable borrowing and a default that shook global markets, the current package aims to cement a sustained period of disinflation and growth, even as austerity measures stir public unrest. In the long view, while such reforms may bolster fiscal credibility and restore investor confidence, they also risk deepening social divides—a challenge that has recurrently defined Argentina’s economic narrative over the past century.
