# Microsoft Paper Ranks 40 U.S. Jobs by ‘AI Applicability’ and Sparks Immediate Industry Ripples

On 29 July 2025 Microsoft Research released a 200-page study scoring every U.S. occupation for AI takeover risk, singling out translators-to-writers as most exposed and manual trades as safest, instantly colouring hiring policies, developer sentiment surveys, and even Meta’s interview process.

#### Focusing Facts

- Study mined 200,000 anonymised Bing Copilot chats to create an ‘AI applicability’ index that put translators, historians, sales reps, writers and customer-service reps at the top of the risk table.

- Bottom of the list—heavy-equipment operators, roofers, housekeepers, massage therapists and surgeons—rated least automatable because tasks require physical dexterity or high-stakes tactile judgment.

- Stack Overflow’s 2025 poll of 49,000 developers shows AI usage up to 84 % but trust down to 33 %, with 66 % citing ‘almost-right’ code as their main headache.

#### Context

Like the power-loom riots of 1811–13 or the 1980s displacement of bank tellers post-ATM (yet teller numbers kept rising until 2005), Microsoft’s list is another flashpoint in the centuries-long dance between automation and labour. The document crystallises a trend that began with 2017’s transformer breakthrough: cognitive, not physical, work is now cheapest to automate. Meta’s move to permit AI-assisted coding tests and Lightcast’s data showing an 800 % jump in non-tech AI-skill postings confirm the market is already re-architecting around augmentation rather than head-count growth, while Stack Overflow’s ‘almost-right’ tax hints at hidden friction costs. Over a 100-year horizon, the episode matters because it formalises a sorting of work into ‘bits-bound’ versus ‘atom-bound’ tasks; if history rhymes, new categories (e.g., AI ethicists, prompt engineers) will emerge, but only societies that, like post-WWII Bretton Woods or China’s newly proposed global AI body, build inclusive governance frameworks are likely to translate productivity gains into broad prosperity rather than entrenched inequality.
