# Chile Passes Major Pension Reform amid Political Gridlock

After decades of political gridlock, Chile's Congress approved a significant reform to the private pension system, increasing employer contributions and raising the guaranteed minimum pension.

#### Focusing Facts

- The bill was approved with 110 votes in favor and 38 votes against in Chile's Congress.

- The reform raises employer contributions from about 1.5% to 8.5% of salaries over nine years.

- The Universal Guaranteed Pension will increase to 250,000 pesos for those 65 and older.

#### Context

Chile's pension reform marks a significant shift from the neoliberal policies established during Pinochet's dictatorship in the 1980s, reflecting an ongoing trend in Latin America to balance market-oriented models with enhanced social security measures. Historically, similar reforms have occurred, such as in Argentina's pension overhaul in 2008, where the government shifted to a state-controlled system. This moment reflects the broader global trend of addressing income inequality and the inadequacies of privatized pension systems. On a centennial timeline, these reforms illustrate the global ebb and flow between privatized market solutions and state interventions in managing retirement security, indicating a persistent equilibrium-seeking process in social welfare policies.
