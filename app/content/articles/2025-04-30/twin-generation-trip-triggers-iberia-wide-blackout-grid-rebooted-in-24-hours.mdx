# Twin Generation Trip Triggers Iberia-Wide Blackout; Grid Rebooted in 24 Hours

On 28 April 2025 two disconnections in southwest Spain knocked 15 GW off-line in five seconds, collapsing the Spain-Portugal grid and forcing a full black-start that restored 99 % of supply by dawn on 29 April.

#### Focusing Facts

- Red Eléctrica’s data show solar PV output diving from >18 GW to ~8 GW between 12:30-12:35 CET, dropping its share from 59 % to 26 %.

- Domestic generation briefly fell to zero across the Iberian Peninsula, stranding 150,000 metro passengers and cutting power to about 60 million people.

- Grid operators stabilized frequency by importing power through France and Morocco interconnectors while hydropower and combined-cycle plants provided restart inertia.

#### Context

Continental blackouts are not new—Italy’s 56 million-person outage in September 2003 and the U.S.–Canada Northeast blackout of 1965 both began with a single line trip that cascaded through low-inertia networks.  What is new is the speed at which modern grids shed inertia as rotating machines are displaced by inverter-based renewables: Iberia’s instantaneous mix exceeded 70 % solar-wind at the failure point, versus less than 5 % two decades earlier.  The episode underscores a systemic tension familiar since Edison’s Pearl Street plant (1882): reliability comes from over-engineering and kinetic buffers, while efficiency and decarbonization pursue the opposite.  Whether Spain installs synthetic-inertia converters, retains nuclear units, or adds storage, the long arc—electrification intertwined with climate constraints—will likely demand hybrid solutions rather than purity campaigns.  On a 100-year timeline, this outage matters less for its brief darkness than for testing how post-fossil grids absorb shocks once taken up by spinning steel.
