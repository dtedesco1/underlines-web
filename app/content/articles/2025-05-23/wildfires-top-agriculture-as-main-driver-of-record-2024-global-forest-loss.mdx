# Wildfires Top Agriculture as Main Driver of Record 2024 Global Forest Loss

Satellite data show 6.7 million ha of primary tropical forest burned in 2024—an 80 % jump from 2023—marking the first year fires, not farming, caused the greatest share of rainforest destruction.

#### Focusing Facts

- Brazil alone lost 2.8 million ha of primary forest in 2024, two-thirds to fire, representing 42 % of global tropical loss.

- Fire-related tree loss released an estimated 4.1 gigatons of CO₂ in 2024—over four times the emissions from all air travel in 2023.

- Bolivia’s primary forest loss soared 200 % to 1.5 million ha, elevating it to the world’s second-largest source of tropical forest loss despite its smaller forest base.

#### Context

What we are witnessing echoes the 1997-98 Indonesian peat fires, when an El Niño-charged drought helped torch 8 million ha and spewed haze across Southeast Asia, but today’s burn scar is far broader—spanning Amazonia to Siberia—and arrives after three decades of repeated climate alarms. The shift from chainsaws to flames signals that planetary warming is beginning to overwhelm even well-intentioned deforestation policies: a systemic feedback loop in which hotter, drier forests ignite more easily, releasing carbon that drives still more heat. In historical terms, forests have buffered human industry since the start of the fossil-fuel era (~1850); if current trajectories hold, by 2125 vast tracts of the Amazon and Congo basins could cross a savannization threshold, subtracting a major carbon sink just as global population peaks. This inflection point matters because it undermines the 2030 Glasgow pledge, the Paris carbon budget, and the very logic of nature-based climate solutions, forcing societies to rethink conservation finance, fire management, and commodity supply chains under a far less predictable climate regime.

#### Narrow Perspectives

- **Left-leaning international media (e.g., The Guardian, The New York Times)**: Portray the record forest loss as a stark, climate-driven emergency that shows humanity is badly off-track on deforestation pledges and must act immediately to avert ecological tipping points. By stressing worst-case phrases such as “frightening” and “global red alert,” they amplify a sense of crisis that can mobilize climate action but can also discount areas of recent progress, like Indonesia’s decline in loss, which gets only brief mention. ([The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2025/may/21/fires-record-loss-forests-global-heating-agriculture-logging-brazil-bolivia-aoe), [The New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/05/22/climate/deforestation-wri-report.html))

- **Policy-and-regulation focused outlets (e.g., POLITICO, Axios)**: Use the same data set to underscore how runaway fires jeopardize climate targets while highlighting stalled or inadequate policy responses, such as the EU’s delay on anti-deforestation rules. Framing centers on political accountability—spotlighting Brussels or Washington—so the narrative can over-simplify complex local drivers and present stricter regulation as the primary remedy. ([POLITICO](https://www.politico.eu/article/wildfires-send-forest-destruction-to-20-year-high-in-dangerous-climate-feedback-loop/), [Axios](https://www.axios.com/2025/05/21/global-forest-loss-record-co2))

- **Global-South and regional media (e.g., Firstpost, The Times of India, Anadolu Ajansı)**: Emphasize how climate-fueled fires are ravaging forests—especially in Brazil and Bolivia—while stressing that developing nations will need new funding mechanisms and adaptation policies to cope. By foregrounding the pain felt in emerging economies, they implicitly call on wealthier nations to foot the bill, which can tilt coverage toward highlighting financing gaps and away from these countries’ own land-use policies.
