# Global Seizure of Lumma Stealer’s 2,300-Domain Command Network

Between May 13-22 2025, a Microsoft-led coalition won a U.S. court order and, with the DOJ, FBI, Europol and others, yanked 2,300+ web domains and five C2 panels that ran the LummaC2 malware-as-a-service, abruptly cutting contact with 394,000 infected Windows PCs.

#### Focusing Facts

- Microsoft telemetry counted 394,000 Lumma-infected Windows systems from 16 Mar to 16 May 2025.

- The U.S. District Court for the Northern District of Georgia authorized Microsoft on 13 May 2025 to seize or sinkhole roughly 2,300 Lumma-related domains.

- FBI data attributes at least 1.7 million credential-theft attempts and $36.5 million in 2023 credit-card losses to Lumma.

#### Context

Big botnet takedowns follow a familiar arc: think Operation Tovar’s 2014 hit on GameOver Zeus or the 2016 Avalanche dismantling—both temporarily choked sprawling crimeware, only for copy-cats to resurface. Lumma’s seizure fits this historical pattern of infrastructure-first disruption, reflecting two long-running trends: (1) crimeware commoditization via malware-as-a-service, which lowers the barrier to entry much as exploit kits did in the 2010s, and (2) the strengthening of public-private cyber task-forces able to cross legal jurisdictions with court-ordered domain sinkholing. On a 100-year horizon the event matters less for the individual malware family than for the precedent: sovereignty over Internet naming and hosting is increasingly wielded as a policing tool, signaling that future cyber conflicts may be fought through rapid legal-technical blockades rather than purely on-net cat-and-mouse code. Whether Lumma’s Russian developer “Shamel” rebuilds or not, the tempo of takedowns versus re-emergence will chart the balance of power between decentralized criminal markets and coordinated state-corporate defense.
