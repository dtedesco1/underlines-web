# Meta–Anduril Seal ‘EagleEye’ AR Combat Helmet Bid for U.S. Army

On 29 May 2025 Meta formally entered the defense-contractor arena, partnering with Palmer Luckey’s Anduril to co-develop the AI-driven EagleEye augmented-reality helmet and other XR gear for U.S. soldiers.

#### Focusing Facts

- The pair have already filed a joint proposal for an Army augmented-reality program worth up to $100 million, according to a Defense Department solicitation and Wall Street Journal reporting.

- Meta’s Reality Labs burned $4.2 billion in Q1 2025 and has soaked up roughly $80 billion since 2014, giving the $1 trillion U.S. defense budget new appeal as a revenue offset.

- Anduril raised $1.5 billion in August 2024 and is reportedly seeking another $2.5 billion, underscoring the torrent of private capital flooding defense tech.

#### Context

Silicon Valley’s swing toward the Pentagon echoes IBM’s 1943 pivot from office machines to bomb-sight computers and Microsoft’s 2008 Joint Enterprise Licensing deals—moments when commercial tech hit wartime demand and never looked back.  The Meta–Anduril pact sits at the confluence of three long arcs: the militarization of dual-use AI, Big Tech’s search for non-advertising revenue as growth stalls, and the normalization of overt political alignment after years of employee pushback (e.g., Google’s Project Maven revolt in 2018).  If XR head-mounted computing becomes standard kit, this collaboration could shift soldier cognition the way the transistor shifted battlefield communications in the 1950s, entwining consumer and defense supply chains for decades.  On a century scale the significance lies less in a single helmet than in codifying AI-augmented perception as state power’s next substrate—potentially redefining who controls, and profits from, the information layer of war.
