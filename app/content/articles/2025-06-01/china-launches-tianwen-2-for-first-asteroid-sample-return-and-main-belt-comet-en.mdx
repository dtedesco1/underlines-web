# China Launches Tianwen-2 for First Asteroid Sample Return and Main-Belt Comet Encounter

On 29 May 2025, a Long March 3B rocket sent the 2.1-ton Tianwen-2 probe toward near-Earth asteroid 469219 Kamoʻoalewa, inaugurating China’s decade-spanning mission to collect extraterrestrial material beyond the Moon and then sling-shot to comet-like body 311P/PanSTARRS.

#### Focusing Facts

- Spacecraft entered an Earth-to-asteroid transfer orbit 18 minutes after liftoff from Xichang at 01:31 Beijing time, successfully deploying fan-shaped solar arrays.

- Mission timeline: arrival at Kamoʻoalewa July 2026; sample capsule return to Earth November 2027 with a target minimum of ≈100 g of regolith.

- A planned Earth gravity assist will redirect the mothership to rendezvous with active asteroid/comet 311P around 2035, making it the first mission to study a main-belt comet up close.

#### Context

China’s bid echoes earlier milestone sample-returns—Japan’s Hayabusa (2003-2010) and NASA’s OSIRIS-REx (2016-2023)—but folds both an asteroid collection and a comet rendezvous into a single flight, reminiscent of the Soviet Union’s multi-leg Luna series in the 1960s-70s. Strategically, it signals Beijing’s shift from lunar confidence-building to full solar-system operations at a moment when U.S. planetary budgets face periodic cuts, eroding the post-Voyager monopoly on deep-space firsts. Over the next century, such autonomous, multi-destination craft will underpin resource prospecting, planetary-defense mapping, and geopolitical soft power beyond Earth orbit; Tianwen-2’s launch therefore matters less for its 100-g of rock than for the precedent of sustained, modular Chinese exploration infrastructure. The mission’s success—or lack thereof—will test whether a state-led, comparatively opaque program can match or surpass the iterative, open-data models that have dominated space science since 1957.
