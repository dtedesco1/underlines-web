# Supreme Court 7-2 Stay Lets Trump Scrap CHNV Parole for 530,000 Migrants

On 30 May 2025 the Supreme Court, in an unsigned 7-2 emergency order, paused a lower-court injunction and cleared the Trump administration to immediately terminate the Biden-era CHNV humanitarian parole program covering roughly half a million Cubans, Haitians, Nicaraguans and Venezuelans.

#### Focusing Facts

- The stay lifts Judge Indira Talwani’s nationwide block, exposing 532,000 CHNV parolees to loss of work permits and potential deportation once their two-year authorizations expire, some as soon as 1 June 2025.

- Justices Ketanji Brown Jackson and Sonia Sotomayor dissented; the other seven justices—including the three Democratic appointees—granted the stay.

- The case, Noem v. Doe, continues in the First Circuit while the Court’s earlier 9 May order already allowed deportation of 350,000 Venezuelans previously shielded by TPS.

#### Context

Since the 1956 Hungarian refugee parole and the 1960-62 Cuban refugee waves, presidents have used humanitarian parole as a safety valve when Congress stalls.  Like the Court’s 2018 Trump v. Hawaii travel-ban decision, this ruling again defers to executive discretion in immigration, reinforcing the 19th-century “plenary power” doctrine that Congress and the President—not judges—set admission rules.  The shadow-docket stay also exemplifies a broader trend: high-impact immigration shifts decided without full briefing, escalating the Court’s role as arbiter of rapid policy swings each time the White House changes hands.  On a century scale, the decision signals that humanitarian admissions can be rescinded en masse, a precedent that may chill future emergency programs just as climate-driven displacement is projected to surge.  It deepens the oscillation between expansion and restriction that has marked U.S. immigration since the Chinese Exclusion Act (1882) and suggests that, absent statutory reform, millions will live in legal limbo subject to electoral whiplash rather than durable legislative consensus.
