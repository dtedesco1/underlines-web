# India’s First ISRO Astronaut Heads to ISS on Commercial Axiom-4 Flight

On 31 May 2025 New Delhi confirmed that Group Captain Shubhanshu Shukla will launch 8 June aboard SpaceX’s Ax-4 Dragon, becoming the first ISRO astronaut on the ISS and carrying seven home-grown microgravity biology experiments.

#### Focusing Facts

- Liftoff set for 8 June 2025, 18:41 IST from Kennedy Space Center LC-39A on a Falcon 9 launching the Dragon capsule to a 14-day ISS stay.

- Shukla will be India’s first ISRO-trained spacefarer and only the nation’s second person in space after Rakesh Sharma (Soyuz T-11, 1984).

- Ax-4 crew plans ~60 studies from 31 countries; India’s package includes algae- and spirulina-based closed-loop food experiments using indigenously built biotech kits.

#### Context

Seen against history, this echoes India’s 1984 Interkosmos ride while swapping Soviet patronage for a pay-as-you-go seat in a U.S.-led commercial mission—much like smaller nations joined NASA’s Shuttle flights in the 1980s or Arabsat financed France’s Ariane in 1985.  The Ax-4 deal signals two intertwined megatrends: (1) low-Earth orbit’s rapid privatization since Ax-1 in 2022, shifting ISS access from geopolitical blocs to market contracts; and (2) India’s pivot from launch-service provider to full-spectrum human-space participant ahead of its indigenous Gaganyaan (target 2027).  Whether this matters in 2125 hinges on today’s baby steps toward closed-loop bioregenerative life support—the microalgae/spirulina work reprises 1960s Biosatellite studies but with vastly better omics tools.  If humanity builds off-world habitats, such research—and the willingness of emerging powers to buy into a shared orbital lab—may mark the era when space science decoupled from super-power budgets and became another line item in national R&D, much like trans-oceanic air travel did after the 1930s.
