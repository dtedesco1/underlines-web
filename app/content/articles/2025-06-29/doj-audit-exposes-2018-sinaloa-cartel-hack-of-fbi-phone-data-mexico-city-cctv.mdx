# DOJ Audit Exposes 2018 Sinaloa Cartel Hack of FBI Phone Data & Mexico City CCTV

A 27 June 2025 inspector-general report revealed that in 2018 a Sinaloa cartel hacker exploited an FBI attaché’s phone metadata and Mexico City’s camera network, enabling the cartel to locate and murder bureau informants.

#### Focusing Facts

- Using the attaché’s phone number, the hacker pulled call logs and geolocation records in real time during 2018.

- Cartel operatives then tapped Mexico City’s ~15,000-camera surveillance grid to tail the agent and identify at least several confidential sources who were later intimidated or killed.

- The Justice Department audit urged expanded ‘ubiquitous technical surveillance’ (UTS) training and a new FBI mitigation plan now under development.

#### Context

Organized crime weaponizing state surveillance echoes Pablo Escobar’s 1989 use of Medellín police radio to ambush rivals and the 2013 Snowden files showing NSA-style geotracking; but this time a non-state actor married commercial phone-data brokers with municipal CCTV.  Over the last two decades, bulk location data has become a commodity, eroding the monopoly governments once held on real-time tracking.  The breach spotlights a structural shift: inexpensive cyber talent and purchasable data let cartels perform intelligence operations once reserved for nation-states, undermining traditional informant-based policing.  On a century horizon, the episode marks another step in the democratization of surveillance power; unless legal and technical guardrails evolve, the balance between state coercion and criminal counter-coercion may tilt further, turning open urban sensor webs into lethal targeting tools rather than public-safety assets.
