# Trump-Mediated Black Sea Ceasefire Hinges on Sanctions Relief

In March 2025, ceasefire talks brokered by Trump administration officials in Saudi Arabia yielded a provisional Black Sea truce between Russia and Ukraine, conditional on easing key Western sanctions.

#### Focusing Facts

- Russian and Ukrainian delegations agreed to a naval ceasefire in the Black Sea during talks on March 25, 2025.

- Russia's implementation of the truce is contingent on lifting sanctions on institutions like Rosselkhozbank and restoring access to the SWIFT system.

- Despite the deal, Russia continues strikes in Ukraine, and the ceasefire remains fragile with differing accounts from US, Russian, and Ukrainian officials.

#### Context

This ceasefire development echoes historical instances where temporary pauses in conflict masked deeper unresolved issues. For instance, the 1953 Korean Armistice halted active fighting without resolving the underlying hostilities, much like how today’s Black Sea truce offers only a provisional respite conditioned on economic and political concessions. The negotiations, influenced by a reoriented US policy under Trump that many see as shifting traditional alliances, suggest a long-term trend where economic leverage is tied to military de-escalation. Over a century, such conditional truces have often been stepping stones to either prolonged détente or renewed conflict, raising questions about the durability of global order in a multipolar world.

- **Left leaning media**: They portray Trump’s approach to negotiating a ceasefire and concessions as dangerously favoring Russia, undermining Ukraine’s security and democratic values. This perspective may overemphasize the negative implications of any diplomatic concessions and be predisposed to critique Trump’s strategy regardless of its potential benefits. This viewpoint might downplay Ukraine’s concerns and the long-term risks by focusing on immediate diplomatic progress and portraying concessions as necessary compromises.
